01 — Overview

Two Sources, One Lesson

This project combines two assignments: a tariff analysis (the "Ridiculous Tariff in Italy" document) and a creative writing exercise (Story of Trade: Venice 1608). Together they grounded the abstract concept of trade barriers in real, tangible consequences.

The Italy tariff assignment asked me to find and explain a historically absurd trade barrier and analyse why it existed. The story exercise asked me to narrate, in first person, the economic consequences of a tariff on a real merchant. Both assignments forced me to see trade barriers not as policy abstractions but as decisions with winners and losers.

02 — The Venice Story (My Assignment)

Venice, 1608 — A Merchant's Misfortune

Story of Trading · Venezia 1608

Since this story concerns trade, it only seemed right to set it in Venice. There was once a merchant who, returning from a year-long voyage, discovered that a tariff of 3 Lire had been imposed on his Indian spices to enter the city.

Rather than pay, the furious merchant turned to his cousin: "Let me through the back way, through another canal — no checkpoints."

The unlucky merchant was caught anyway — and paid a fine of 300 Lire... If only he had paid the original tariff.

Moral: evading a trade barrier costs far more than complying with it — and barriers distort behaviour in unpredictable ways.

I wrote this story in Italian and set it in Venice deliberately — Venice was a major trading hub where tariffs and customs duties were a real part of daily commerce. The story captures how a small tariff (3 Lire) creates an incentive to evade, and how that evasion compounds the cost enormously (300 Lire fine). This illustrates a genuine economic phenomenon: tariffs create deadweight loss beyond just the tariff itself by distorting behaviour.

03 — The Italy Tariff Analysis

52% on Cotton Textiles — Why Was It Ridiculous?

For the tariff analysis assignment, I examined Italy's 52% tariff on printed cotton textiles. This was my analysis of why it existed — and why it was considered extreme even at the time.

Winner
Italian Textile Producers
Protected from cheaper foreign competition. With a 52% tariff on imports, domestic producers could charge higher prices without losing customers. The tariff created an artificial competitive advantage.
Loser
Italian Consumers
Paid significantly more for cotton goods than the world market price. The tariff acted as a hidden tax redistributed from consumers to producers. Total economic efficiency fell.

My analysis noted that even in protectionist countries of the era, 40% was already considered high. The 52% rate was so extreme it raised the price of imported cotton goods to the point where domestic demand collapsed — meaning the protection ultimately hurt the very producers it was designed to help, because consumers bought less overall.

04 — Evidence

From My Assignments

Tariff Analysis — Italy Cotton

Cotton goods tariff: 52% on printed textiles. Purpose: to protect local textile production — higher tariffs on imported textile reduces consumption of foreign imports. Even in protectionist countries, 40% was already considered high.

Story of Trade — Venice 1608

Original story written in Italian. Setting: Venice (major historical trading hub). Plot: merchant discovers 3-Lira tariff on Indian spices, attempts to evade via back canal, is caught and fined 300 Lire. Key insight: evasion costs far exceed compliance costs.

Why These Two Assignments Belong Together

The tariff analysis explains the theory — why barriers exist and who benefits. The Venice story shows the behavioural distortions — the incentive to evade creates additional economic damage beyond the tariff itself. Together they cover both the policy rationale and the human cost.

05 — What Did I Learn?

Knowledge

Tariffs protect producers at the expense of consumers and overall economic efficiency. The deadweight loss — the value destroyed by reduced trade — falls on no one specifically, which is why tariffs persist politically even when they damage aggregate welfare.

Skills

I can now decompose the effects of a tariff into: government revenue, producer gain, consumer loss, and deadweight loss. I can identify who lobbies for tariffs (producers) and who loses silently (consumers — dispersed and unorganised).

Reflection

The story exercise surprised me — writing fiction about economics forced me to internalise the concepts more deeply than any formula. The merchant's 300-Lire fine made "deadweight loss from evasion" concrete and unforgettable. Real-world tariff debates always feel different to me now.
