01 — Overview

Why Nations Trade — and How We Predict It

This project is based on the first reading of the course, which covered the foundations of international economics and introduced the seven recurring themes that structure the entire field. The key practical tool from this reading was the Gravity Model — a surprisingly simple formula that predicts how much two countries will trade with each other.

Understanding this model matters because it explains real-world trade patterns: why Germany trades more with France than with Vietnam, why size amplifies trade, and why distance — not just geographical but cultural and political — is a genuine barrier. It is used by governments to design trade policy and by economists to forecast the effects of agreements like Brexit.

03 — Evidence from My Work

Notes from Reading 1

From my notes — Guiding Questions

I structured my reading around three questions: What are the 7 recurring themes? What factors does the Gravity Model use? How has the pattern of trade changed over time?

From my notes — Gravity Model Formula

The model uses two inputs: the size of each economy (GDP) and the distance between them. Larger economies generate more trade; greater distance reduces it. The formula produces a "gravity score" used to benchmark actual trade flows.

From my notes — Limitations

The Gravity Model fails when cultural ties aren't captured (e.g. Ireland–UK), when trade agreements exist, or when political conflicts override economic incentives. Distance alone doesn't explain everything.

From my notes — Historical Shift

Trade has shifted from primary products (raw materials) toward manufactured goods, and more trade now occurs between distant countries as globalisation reduces effective distance through technology and logistics.

04 — What Did I Learn?

Knowledge

International economics differs from domestic economics because sovereign countries cannot be forced to cooperate. The seven themes frame every policy debate — from tariff design to exchange rate intervention.

Skills

I learned to read the Gravity Model as a diagnostic tool: if two large, nearby countries trade less than the model predicts, something — a political barrier, cultural mismatch, or historical tension — is suppressing natural trade.

Reflection

I was surprised that distance still matters so much in an age of the internet. The model reminded me that physical logistics, time zones, and cultural proximity remain very real costs even in globalised supply chains.
