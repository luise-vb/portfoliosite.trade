The autarky debate forced me to argue both sides of one of the oldest questions in international economics: is self-sufficiency a strength or a trap? The answer is deeply contextual — and that is exactly the point.

01 — Overview

The Oldest Question in Trade Policy

This project comes from a debate activity in which I engaged with AI (ChatGPT) to argue both sides of the autarky debate. Autarky means a policy of national economic self-sufficiency — producing everything domestically and minimising dependence on foreign trade. The opposite position — deep trade integration — maximises efficiency but creates strategic dependencies.

The assignment was particularly valuable because it forced me to build the strongest possible case for each side, rather than simply accepting the free-trade consensus of mainstream economics. The debate revealed that the right answer genuinely depends on what you are most afraid of: economic inefficiency, or strategic vulnerability.

02 — The Debate

The Best Arguments on Each Side

The Case For Autarky
Self-Sufficiency as Strength
◆
National Security: Dependence on foreign suppliers for critical goods — food, energy, pharmaceuticals, semiconductors — creates strategic vulnerabilities that can be weaponised by adversaries or disrupted by crises.
◆
Supply Chain Resilience: COVID-19 demonstrated that globally integrated supply chains can collapse suddenly. Countries that maintained domestic production capacity were more resilient.
◆
Industrial Policy: Domestic industries cannot develop if immediately undercut by cheaper foreign competition. Temporary protection allows infant industries to scale.
◆
Employment: Offshoring production destroys domestic jobs and communities. The social cost of deindustrialisation is real even when aggregate GDP rises.
The Case For Trade Openness
Interdependence as Stability
◈
Efficiency: Comparative advantage means countries produce at lower opportunity cost when they specialise. Autarky forces countries to produce goods they are bad at, permanently lowering living standards.
◈
Peacekeeping: Countries that trade heavily with each other have strong economic incentives not to go to war. Economic interdependence is one of the most robust predictors of peace.
◈
Innovation: Open economies access global knowledge, technology, and competition. Closed economies fall behind because they lack the spur of foreign competition and the scale of global markets.
◈
Consumer Welfare: Protection raises prices. Autarky is a permanent tax on consumers, who pay more for everything to subsidise domestic producers.

03 — Country Case Studies

What History Shows

North Korea
Near-Total Autarky
Extreme isolation has produced one of the world's poorest economies. Food insecurity is chronic. The regime survives but citizens bear enormous welfare costs.
Outcome: Very Poor
India (1947–1991)
Import Substitution
Protected heavy industry from foreign competition. Growth was slow and productivity lagged. The 1991 liberalisation triggered decades of rapid growth.
Outcome: Below Potential
Singapore
Maximum Openness
No natural resources, tiny domestic market. Became one of the wealthiest nations per capita by maximising trade openness and foreign investment.
Outcome: Excellent
USA (2018–)
Strategic Decoupling
Selective protectionism in semiconductors, EVs, and strategic tech. Trade-off between efficiency loss and reduced dependency on adversarial suppliers. Ongoing experiment.
Outcome: Mixed

05 — Evidence

From the Debate Activity

Format of the Assignment

I used ChatGPT to simulate a structured debate, arguing both sides of the autarky question. The AI pushed back on weak arguments and forced me to strengthen each position with specific economic reasoning.

My Key Insight from the Debate

The autarky debate has no clean answer because it depends on the threat model. If you fear economic stagnation, free trade wins. If you fear strategic vulnerability or war, some degree of self-sufficiency becomes rational. The debate showed me that economics cannot be separated from geopolitics.

Connection to Course Concepts

The autarky debate connects directly to: comparative advantage (what you give up by not specialising), trade policy (which instruments achieve strategic goals), and the Gravity Model (autarky severs the geographic trade flows the model predicts). It served as an integrating exercise for the whole course.

06 — What Did I Learn?

Knowledge

Autarky has never produced prosperity when pursued as a permanent strategy. But selective strategic decoupling in critical sectors — semiconductors, food, energy — is a rational response to genuine security threats, as major economies are now discovering.

Skills

Debating both sides taught me to stress-test arguments. I can now identify when a free-trade argument ignores distributional concerns, and when a protectionist argument ignores efficiency costs. Good trade policy analysis requires holding both simultaneously.

Reflection

This was the most interesting project because it had no right answer. Economics gave me the efficiency argument; political science gave me the security argument; history gave me the evidence. I left the debate more uncertain than I started — but with a much richer framework for thinking about it.
